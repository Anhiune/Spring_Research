1. Introduction 
Semiconductors are materials that regulate electrical current and serve as the foundational 
components of nearly all modern technologies, from smartphones and electric vehicles to medical 
devices, defense systems, and artificial intelligence. Their production depends on a deeply global 
and interdependent supply chain. Key stages such as design, fabrication, and tooling rely on inputs 
from the U.S., Taiwan, Japan, Korea, and China.  In 2022, the United States enacted the CHIPS 
and Science Act, a $52.7 billion initiative aimed at reshoring semiconductor manufacturing, 
enhancing national security, and reducing reliance on Chinese supply chains. In response, China 
expanded its “Big Fund” , a state-led initiative focused on boosting domestic chip manufacturing 
capacity, achieving technological independence, and accelerating innovation in areas like 5nm chip 
production. China also continues to dominate the supply chain for critical upstream resources, 
processing nearly 90% of the world’s rare earth elements, which further strengthens its position in 
the industry. This research evaluates the effectiveness of both nations’ strategies within the context 
of global supply chain dynamics and economic competition. Findings indicate that the CHIPS Act, 
though strategically motivated, may be inadvertently undermining U.S. competitiveness by driving 
up costs, delaying production, and straining supply chain access , while China has accelerated 
progress toward self-reliance. Ultimately, the study argues that U.S. leadership in semiconductors 
requires not isolation but greater investment in innovation, talent development, and global 
cooperation, especially in a sector where no nation can succeed in isolation. 
2. Literature Review 
The semiconductor industry has attracted significant academic and policy attention for its 
foundational role in global economic development, technological innovation, and national 
security. A growing body of literature highlights the industry's rapid techn ological evolution, 
intricate global supply chains, dependence on critical raw materials, and escalating geopolitical 
tensions. Semiconductors power virtually all modern electronics, and the exponential increase in 
computing capabilities over the past seve ral decades has been driven by Moore’s Law , the 
observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubles approximately every two years. As 
Waldrop (2016) notes, this scaling has underpinned innovation across sectors such as artificial 

intelligence and consumer electronics. Yet, reports by the International Technology Roadmap for 
Semiconductors (ITRS, 2022) emphasize the growing complexity of maintaining this pace as 
manufacturing nodes approach the atomic scale, with 2nm and 1nm technol ogies anticipated by 
2027. 
At the same time, the semiconductor supply chain has become increasingly fragmented and 
globally interdependent. Gereffi (2019) documents how design is concentrated in the United 
States, fabrication in East Asia, packaging in Southeast Asia, and critical equipment production in 
countries like Japan and the Netherlands. Baldwin and Evenett (2020) argue that while this 
distribution allows for specialization and efficiency, it introduces vulnerabilities to regional and 
geopolitical disruptions. Data from the S emiconductor Industry Association (2023) and Visual 
Capitalist (2023) indicate that over 70% of advanced chips are fabricated in East Asia, with 
Taiwan’s TSMC alone responsible for more than half of global foundry revenue. Compounding 
this structural risk is the industry’s heavy reliance on a narrow set of raw materials. Studies by 
Humphries (2013) and Mancheri et al. (2021) point to China's dominance in the supply of rare 
earth elements, gallium, and germanium , materials essential for chip fabrication. In response to 
recent U.S. policies like the CHIPS Act, China has imposed export restrictions on gallium and 
germanium (Financial Times, 2023), raising alarms about material access and supply chain 
resilience. Calls for diversifying material sources through domestic mining and allied cooperation 
are echoed in reports by the U.S. Geological Survey (2022) and the Center for Strategic and 
International Studies (CSIS, 2023). 
Despite its cyclical volatility, the semiconductor sector remains a core driver of global 
economic growth. McKinsey (2022) forecasts the industry to reach $1 trillion in annual revenue 
by 2030, with demand continuing to rise in automotive technologies, art ificial intelligence, and 
advanced consumer devices. Sectoral analyses by Vyrian (2023) and Statista (2023) confirm these 
trends, while Visual Capitalist (2023) ranks Intel, TSMC, and Samsung among the industry’s 
leading revenue generators. However, recent disruptions in production and logistics, such as those 
noted by ElectroIQ (2023), underscore the fragility of global operations and the need for greater 
structural resilience. Geopolitical dynamics have further heightened the strategic salience of 
semiconductor production. Analysts such as Kaplan (2021) and institutions like the Center for 
Security and Emerging Technology (CSET, 2023) emphasize that technological leadership in 

semiconductors lies at the heart of U.S.- China competition. Export controls on EUV lithography 
tools by the United States and allied nations are intended to restrict China’s ability to advance in 
cutting-edge fabrication. RAND (2022) warns that geographic concentration in Taiwan which 
produces over 60% of the world’s advanced chips represents a major strategic vulnerability in the 
current global configuration. 
In response, governments across the world have launched national initiatives aimed at securing 
semiconductor capabilities and reducing reliance on foreign sources. The United States’ CHIPS 
and Science Act (2022) allocates $52.7 billion to support domestic manufacturing and research 
infrastructure (White House Fact Sheet, 2022). The European Union’s Chips Act aims to double 
regional production capacity by 2030 through $47 billion in targeted investments (European 
Commission, 2022). China’s “Big Fund” has mobilized over $48 billion in two phases to develop 
self-sufficient chip ecosystems (China Briefing, 2023). In parallel, countries such as India, Japan, 
and South Korea are offering incentive packages to attract fabrication and advanced packaging 
facilities ( StartUs Insights, 2025). These coordinated policy responses signal a shift from the 
traditional model of global cost optimization toward one that prioritizes strategic autonomy and 
technological sovereignty. 
While existing research provides valuable insights into distinct aspects of the semiconductor 
industry, such as technological scaling (Waldrop, 2016; ITRS, 2022), supply chain geography 
(Gereffi, 2019; Baldwin & Evenett, 2020), and material dependencies (Humphries, 2013; 
Mancheri et al., 2021) , many of these analyses examine these components in isolation. What is 
often missing is a holistic evaluation of the strategic effectiveness and economic viability of 
national semiconductor policies in the context of global interdependence. This study aims to 
address that gap by critically assessing the consequences of industrial policy initiatives such as the 
CHIPS Act, China’s Big Fund, and the EU Chips Act. Rather than merely describing policy 
objectives, it evaluat es their real -world implications for cost structures, supply chain durability, 
chokepoint vulnerabilities, and global power relations. This framing situates semiconductor policy 
as a form of economic statecraft—one that can either foster resilience and innovation or entrench 
inefficiencies and strategic fragmentation. The study asks whether these interventions are 
genuinely reshoring production or primarily subsidizing entrenched players, and whether they 
enhance long-term security or inadvertently increase  systemic risk. In doing so, it contributes a 

critical lens to the discourse on technological sovereignty and economic security, offering 
guidance for scholars, economists, and policymakers navigating the evolving semiconductor 
landscape. 
 
  

3. Foundations of the Semiconductor Industry 
3.1 Properties and Significance of Semiconductors 
Semiconductors are materials whose electrical conductivity falls between that of 
conductors, such as copper, and insulators, such as ceramics. This unique characteristic , where 
conductivity can be precisely controlled by introducing impurities (doping), applying electric 
fields, or manipulating temperature , makes semiconductors the fundamental building blocks of 
modern electronics. Their ability to regulate electrical flow under specific conditions enables the 
functionality of transistors, diodes, and integ rated circuits, which are the core elements in nearly 
all digital technologies. As such, semiconductors underpin a vast array of critical systems and 
industries. In consumer electronics, the power processors and memory components in 
smartphones, laptops, televisions, and gaming consoles. In the automotive sector, semiconductors 
facilitate engine control, infotainment systems, and increasingly sophisticated driver -assistance 
technologies. In h ealthcare, they are essential to the operation of diagnostic imagi ng tools, 
pacemakers, and biomedical monitoring equipment. In renewable energy, semiconductors enable 
efficient power conversion in solar inverters, battery management systems, and grid -connected 
storage solutions. In the emerging Internet of Things (IoT) ecosystem, they support connectivity 
and computational functionality in everything from smart homes and wearables to industrial sensor 
networks.

3.2 Historical Evolution of the Semiconductor Industry 
The evolution of the semiconductor industry has mirrored , and enabled , the rapid 
transformation of the global technological landscape. Initial scientific insights into the peculiar 
conductive properties of materials like selenium and germanium in the 19th century laid the 
groundwork for subsequent breakthroughs. The invention of the transistor in 1947 at Bell Labs by 
John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley was a pivotal moment, replacing bulky and 
inefficient vacuum tubes with compact, energy-efficient alternatives. This innovation marked the 
birth of modern electronics. The subsequent development of the first silicon-based transistors and 
integrated circuits (ICs) in the 1950s and 1960s, led by Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce, created the 
foundation for exponential increases in processing complexity and miniaturization. From the 
1970s onward, photolithography and fabrication advances allowed industry to shrink transistor 
dimensions further, enabling the rise of high -performance microprocessors, dynamic random -


access memory (DRAM), flash memory, and system -on-chip (SoC) designs that are now 
ubiquitous in advanced digital systems. 
A central force driving semiconductor progress has been Moore’s Law—Gordon Moore’s 
1965 prediction that the number of transistors on an integrated circuit would double approximately 
every two years. This empirical trend has guided industry roadmaps for decades, yielding dramatic 
improvements in computational power, energy efficiency, and cost per transistor. The progression 
from early process nodes exceeding 20 microns in the mid-20th century to today’s advanced nodes 
measuring 7nm, 5nm, and soon 3nm or even 2nm has enabled compact, high-speed chips used in 
everything from mobile devices to data centers. Continued scaling has offered a range of benefits: 
smaller transistors switch faster, consume less power, and occupy less space, allowing more logic 
units to be packed into a single chip. This has been critical for enabling breakthroughs in artificial 
intelligence, machine learning, autonomous systems, and real-time data processing at the network 
edge. 
However, as feature sizes approach atomic dimensions, the semiconductor industry is 
encountering physical, technical, and economic limits. At these scales, quantum tunneling, leakage 
currents, and heat density become increasingly difficult to manage, while  the precision required 
for manufacturing demands extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography and advanced metrology. The 
cost of research, development, and fabrication infrastructure rises sharply with each generation, 
prompting concerns about the long -term feasibility of classical scaling. In response, the industry 
is diversifying its innovative strategies. Rather than relying solely on transistor miniaturization, 
firms are investing in heterogeneous integration, advanced packaging, 3D chip stacking, and new 
materials such as silicon carbide and gallium nitride. These approaches aim to enhance sy stem-
level performance, efficiency, and flexibility, particularly for applications in high -performance 
computing, power electronics, and aerospace systems. 
Semiconductors have thus evolved from niche scientific curiosities to strategic enablers of the 
digital economy, shaping virtually every sector of modern life. Their historical trajectory 
underscores not only their technological importance but also their g eopolitical and economic 
relevance. As Moore’s Law approaches its limits, sustaining progress will require coordinated 
innovation across materials science, circuit design, manufacturing techniques, and global supply 

chain strategies. This foundational understanding of semiconductor physics and industry evolution 
provides essential context for evaluating contemporary policy responses, including the CHIPS and 
Science Act, and international efforts to secure leadership and resilience in this critical domain.

5. Policy Analysis of the CHIPS Act and Strategic U.S. 
Objectives 
5.1 Introduction 
The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 represents a landmark shift in U.S. industrial policy, 
designed to revitalize domestic semiconductor manufacturing, reduce strategic dependence on 
foreign suppliers, and secure the nation’s technological leadership in a rapidly evolving 
geopolitical landscape. The legislation allocates $52.7 billion in federal support, comprising $39 
billion in direct subsidies for semiconductor fabrication facility construction and expansion, $13.2 
billion for research and workforce development initiatives, and a 25% investment tax credit aimed 
at incentivizing capital expenditures in semiconductor manufacturing equipment and 
infrastructure. Beyond the $39 billion in manufacturing incentiv es, the CHIPS Act allocates $11 
billion for research and development in next -generation technologies such as AI, quantum 
computing, advanced packaging , supporting regional innovation hubs and public -private 
collaboration. Smaller but strategically important funds include $2 billion to secure defense-related 
semiconductor access, $1.5 billion to strengthen wireless technologies like Open RAN, $500 
million for international supply chain coordination, and $200 million to expand STEM educa tion 
and vocational training. As Forrester emphasizes, the Act is more than a stimulus package , it is a 
long-term industrial policy aimed at ensuring U.S. technological leadership and resilience amid 
global supply chain interdependence.  To prevent the unin tended diffusion of strategic 
technologies, the Act incorporates guardrails restricting recipients from building advanced 
fabrication plants, defined as those under 28nm , in China for a ten -year period. It also prohibits 
joint ventures and licensing arrangements with designated nations while allowing conditional 
maintenance of legacy operations. These restrictions reflect broader strategic aims: enhancing 
supply chain resil ience, maintaining U.S. dominance in critical technologies such as artificial 
intelligence and quantum computing, and mitigating national security risks linked to 
overdependence on concentrated nodes of global production, particularly Taiwan.

5.2 Policy Evaluation 
Despite its bold ambitions, the CHIPS Act faces significant challenges. China’s response, 
characterized by a determined push toward semiconductor self-sufficiency, raises critical concerns 
about the policy’s effectiveness. Between 2022 and 2024, China reduced its integrated circuit 
imports by nearly 70%, ramping up domestic capabilities through initiatives like the $48 billion 
Big Fund Phase III and progressing in 5nm fabrication using self -aligned quadruple patterning 
(SAQP). Rather than curbing Chinese advancement, the Act may be reinforcing its resolve. Further 
complicating the U.S. effort is its dependence on rare earth elements, of which China refines over 
85% globally. In retaliation to U.S. sanctions, China imposed export restrictions on gallium and 
germanium, two materials essential for chip production , exposing U.S. fabrication to input price 
volatility and supply insecurity. The globalized nature of the semiconductor ecosystem also 
presents structural risks to the reshoring strategy. Semiconductor production requires high -
precision equipment from firms like ASML in the Netherlands, specialty materials from Japan and 
South Korea, and labor -intensive packaging processes concentrated in Southeast Asia. By 


prioritizing national self-reliance, the Act risks disrupting established trade flows, increasing costs, 
and straining alliances. 
Cost-related challenges are particularly acute. U.S. fabrication facilities are estimated to cost two 
to three times more to build and operate than their counterparts in Asia. These cost differentials 
are exacerbated by tariffs on imported tools and materi als, diminishing the competitive viability 
of domestic production even with substantial subsidies. Additionally, the Act has raised concerns 
about corporate welfare, as it includes limited accountability mechanisms. With few claw  back 
provisions and vague success metrics, there is a risk that public funds could disproportionately 
benefit large incumbents without generating corresponding strategic gains. Administrative 
complexity further hampers implementation; the program spans multiple agencies, and the 
timelines for constructing fabs and building a skilled workforce can extend five to ten years, 
delaying material impacts. Workforce shortages added to this delay. The industry faces an 
anticipated shortfall of 67,000 skilled workers by 2030, a problem compou nded by immigration 
restrictions and limited domestic talent pipelines. Without a coordinated national effort to train and 
attract technical labor, capacity investments may go underutilized. Moreover, the inherently 
cyclical nature of semiconductor demand introduces additional risk. Investments in legacy nodes 
may become obsolete amid rapid innovation cycles, reducing the potential return on federal 
spending. The lack of international policy alignment also threatens to fragment the global 
semiconductor strategy. As U.S. allies pursue their own national subsidies, the risk of overcapacity 
and inefficiency increases, underscoring the need for multilateral coordination. 
These complexities fuel an ongoing theoretical debate regarding the appropriate balance between 
market forces and state intervention. While critics argue the CHIPS Act distorts resource allocation 
and invites political lobbying, proponents contend that sem iconductors constitute a high -barrier 
strategic sector warranting public investment. Similarly, while some view the Act’s export controls 
and collaboration restrictions as necessary tools of economic security, others caution that such 
protectionist measure s may stifle international innovation and undermine the interconnected 
research ecosystem that has historically driven technological progress. Nonetheless, there are 
compelling arguments in support of the Act’s objectives. From a national security perspective, the 
United States is heavily reliant on Taiwan, which produces over 90% of the world’s most advanced 
chips. Reshoring capabilities ensure the availability of semiconductors for defense and 

infrastructure applications. International competition also justifies the intervention, as nations such 
as South Korea, China, and the European Union have collectively pledged hundreds of billions in 
subsidies to expand domestic semiconductor production. To remain competitive, U.S. firms require 
comparable support. 
On a regional level, the Act has catalyzed significant private and public investment. States like 
Arizona and New York have developed infrastructure and workforce development programs in 
partnership with local governments and educational institutions. Federal funding supports research 
and innovation through centers such as the National Semiconductor Technology Center (NSTC), 
while the National Science Foundation has increased investment in STEM education to expand 
the future talent pool. Case studies illustrate both the scale and the challenges of implementation: 
Intel has committed over $100 billion in Ohio and Arizona, but encountered delays and financial 
setbacks; TSMC’s $65 billion Arizona project has achieved production milestones but suffered 
early losses; Samsung’s $40 billion investment in Texas remains in early stages amid supply chain 
difficulties; Micron’s $20 billion effort in New York and Idaho has sparked environmental scrutiny; 
and Wolf speed’s $5 billion silicon carbide facility in North Carolina continues construction. These 
initiatives signal momentum but also reveal systemic barriers, cost inefficiencies, talent gaps, and 
global frictions, that threaten to limit long-term success. The CHIPS and Science Act represents a 
historic investment in national economic security, but fulfilling its promise will require not only 
capital and infrastructure, but sustained public-private coordination, international engagement, and 
robust mechanisms for accountability and innovation. 
  

6. Policy Analysis of the Made in China 2025 and the 
National Integrated Circuit Fund 
6.1 Introduction 
In direct response to the United States’ semiconductor industrial policy, most notably the 
CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 , China has significantly intensified its pursuit of technological 
self-reliance through a state -driven industrial strategy grounded in national security imperatives 
and long-term strategic planning. Two primary frameworks anchor China’s approach: the "Made 
in China 2025" (MIC 2025) initiative, which identifies semiconductors as a core sector and sets a 
target of achieving 70% domestic content in critical technologies by 2025, an d the National 
Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund, commonly known as the “Big Fund.” This fund has 
evolved through successive phases : Phase I (2014 –2019) invested $21.8 billion in foundational 
chip production and industrial expansion; Phase II (2019–2024) deployed $29.08 billion to support 
wafer fabrication and key national champions; and Phase III (2024 –2029) earmarks $47.5 billion 
to scale domestic semiconductor equipment and bolster strategic enterprises. Since 2022, China 
has achieved a series of technological breakthroughs that challenge the assumptions underpinning 
U.S. policy. Most notably, Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation (SMIC) 
succeeded in producing 7nm chips for Huawei’s Mate 60 Pro using deep ultraviolet (DUV) 
lithography, despite being barred from accessing extreme ultraviolet (EUV) tools due to U.S. -led 
export controls. Through advanced patterning and computational lithography, Chinese engineers 
circumvented technical limitations previously believed insurmountable. Furthermore, 
experimental deployment of Self -Aligned Quadruple Patterning (SAQP) has allowed Chinese 
firms to reach pilot -scale production at the 5nm level, reflecting a capacity to innovate under 
constraint. 
6.2 Technological Breakthroughs Since 2022 
These advances have been paralleled by a rapid expansion of China’s domestic tooling and 
electronic design automation (EDA) ecosystem. Firms such as Empyrean (EDA software), Naura 
Technology, and AMEC (fabrication equipment) have emerged as viable national  alternatives, 
supported by over 100 newly launched initiatives financed by the Big Fund and provincial 

subsidies. This surge, triggered by U.S. export restrictions, has inadvertently accelerated China’s 
technological independence in critical supply chain segments. Additionally, from 2023 to 2025, 
China initiated construction of more than 18 semiconductor fabrication facilities, concentrating on 
mature-node technologies (28nm and above) for automotive, telecommunications, and Internet -
of-Things (IoT) applications. These fabs, sustained by local incentives and workforce development 
programs, position China to dominate mature-node chip production by 2027 , at a time when the 
U.S. CHIPS Act remains in its early implementation phase. A comparative evaluation of policy 
outcomes reveals clear divergences: while the CHIPS Act sought to block China’s access to 
advanced nodes, restrict tooling and software capabili ties, and secure U.S. fab leadership, China 
has progressed with 7nm manufacturing, 5nm experimentation, a rapidly growing domestic tooling 
ecosystem, and large-scale fab deployment, ultimately deepening its self-reliance and fragmenting 
global supply chains. 
Nonetheless, China continues to face substantial structural constraints. The country 
remains dependent on foreign sources for several critical materials and tools, including 
photoresists from Japan, silicon wafers from Taiwan and South Korea, and high-precision etching 
tools from the United States, Japan, and the Netherlands. Coordinated export restrictions imposed 
by these nations, particularly on EUV and advanced DUV equipment, have the potential to cap 
China’s near-term progress at the 5nm –7nm level unle ss breakthroughs in domestic tooling are 
achieved. Furthermore, issues of governance and investment inefficiency persist. Between 2022 
and 2023, corruption investigations targeting senior Big Fund officials exposed the risks of 
centralized capital allocation without adequate transparency. Several high-profile failures, such as 
the collapse of the Wuhan Hongxin project, underscore the need for more robust oversight and 
performance monitoring. Compounding these challenges is a persistent talent bottleneck. Despite 
a growing pool of STEM graduates, China faces a shortage of experienced engineers in system -
on-chip (SoC) architecture, yield optimization, and advanced process integration. The effects of 
brain drain, restrictions on international academic collabora tion, and limitations on attracting 
foreign-trained professionals continue to constrain domestic capabilities. 
While the CHIPS and Science Act aimed to contain and outpace China’s semiconductor 
ambitions, it may have inadvertently catalyzed Beijing’s strategic acceleration toward self -
sufficiency. China’s ability to adapt through unconventional technological pathways and mobilize 

capital at scale, particularly in mature-node production, demonstrates resilience and state capacity. 
However, enduring vulnerabilities in materials, tools, and human capital expose the limits of rapid 
scaling under constraint. These developments invite deeper reflection on the strategic calculus 
underpinning semiconductor decoupling. Rather than a zero-sum race, a multipolar semiconductor 
order, supported by coordinated investment, transparent governance, and selective collaboration , 
may ultimately offer a more resilient and stable global architecture for technological progress. For 
the United States and its allies, future policy design must weigh the risks of rigid containment 
against the long-term benefits of structured interdependence in securing a balanced and innovative 
semiconductor ecosystem.

7.2 Conclusion 
Semiconductors are the backbone of the modern digital economy, powering everything 
from smartphones and medical devices to defense systems and artificial intelligence infrastructure. 
As such, leadership in semiconductor technology is not only a matter of economic competitiveness, 
but also a critical pillar of national security and strategic autonomy for the United States. However, 
this leadership cannot be sustained without recognizing the deeply interconnected nature of the 
semiconductor manufacturing and raw material supply chains. Semiconductor fabrication depends 
on a globally distributed ecosystem of inputs, processes, and logistics. Raw materials such as 
silicon wafers, gallium, germanium, photoresists, and high purity etching chemicals are sourced 
from a concentrated set of international suppliers. China, for example, dominates the refining of 
rare earth elements and key materials like gallium and germanium; Japan is a global leader in 
photoresist production; and countries such as Ger many and the Netherlands provide essential 
chemicals and metrology equipment. This intricate upstream network means that even if chip 
production is physically reshored, it remains functionally dependent on international flows of 
materials and equipment. Any disruption, whether due to export restrictions, natural disasters, or 
geopolitical conflict, can cascade across the entire value chain. Therefore, building semiconductor 
resilience requires a holistic strategy that includes secure access to raw materials, diversifying 
sources, and deepening global partnerships across every layer of the supply chain. 
The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 marks a pivotal shift in the trajectory of U.S. industrial 
policy, aiming to restore domestic semiconductor manufacturing, safeguard national security, and 
reassert technological leadership in the face of mounting geopolit ical and economic uncertainty. 

At its core, the Act represents a long-term strategic investment rather than a short-term economic 
stimulus, designed to rebuild critical infrastructure, catalyze high -tech innovation, and reduce 
dependence on fragile global supply chains. However, the CHIPS Act now stands at a crossroads, 
confronted by a paradox that threatens to undermine its long -term effectiveness: semiconductors 
are embedded in a globally interdependent ecosystem that cannot be reshored in isolation. Despite 
the infusion of over $52 bi llion in federal incentives and the emergence of major U.S. -based 
projects, the domestic semiconductor industry continues to rely heavily on foreign sources for 
essential raw materials such as gallium and advanced wafers, high -precision lithography 
equipment, and critical technical expertise. In some cases, U.S. tariffs on imported semiconductor 
tools, ranging from 20% to 32% , have compounded cost structures, weakening the price 
competitiveness of domestic fabrication efforts relative to established Asian counterparts. 
While the CHIPS Act has succeeded in mobilizing public and private capital, evidenced by 
large-scale projects from Intel, TSMC, Samsung, and Micron , these efforts have encountered 
delays, financial losses, and operational challenges. Intel’s Ohio project, for instance, has faced 
major setbacks, with factory completion now postponed until the next decade. TSMC’s Arizona 
facility, although achieving production yield milestones exceeding those in Taiwan, has reported 
cumulative losses of over $1.2 billion over  several years. These realities reflect deeper structural 
inefficiencies that federal subsidies alone cannot resolve. At the same time, the U.S. policy 
approach has generated unintended global consequences. China, in particular, has responded 
forcefully by intensifying its push for self -reliance, introducing export restrictions on rare earth 
elements vital to both semiconductor and defense applications, and making significant strides in 
5nm chip development through Self-Aligned Quadruple Patterning (SAQP). Ironically, instead of 
slowing its rivals, the CHIPS Act may have catalyzed a global race toward technological 
independence, triggering fragmented supply chains, subsidy competitions, and reduced global 
efficiency. 
These developments reveal the limitations of a zero-sum framing in semiconductor policy. 
While reshoring key segments of the value chain is strategically prudent, true leadership in 
semiconductors cannot be achieved through isolation. A technologically dom inant nation must 
also serve as a collaborative global partner. The strength of the U.S. semiconductor industry has 
long depended not just on fabrication, but on its leadership in research, talent development, and 

international cooperation. As such, a recalibrated vision for the CHIPS Act is essential —one that 
emphasizes resilience through interdependence, not decoupling. This reorientation begins with a 
deeper commitment to the next-generation R&D in fields like AI chips, quantum computing, and 
advanced packaging technologies. University -industry collaboration must be strengthened to 
ensure that public investment translates into commercial breakthroughs. Simultaneously, talent 
development remains a critical bottleneck. With a projected shortfall of 67,000 skilled workers by 
2030, the U.S. must expand STEM education, vocational training, and pathways for foreign -
trained professionals. 
Moreover, allied coordination is no longer optional but imperative. The United States 
should intensify strategic cooperation with countries such as Japan, South Korea, and the European 
Union to harmonize industrial policies, align export controls, and prevent a counterproductive race 
to the bottom in global subsidies. Smart supply chain design will also be vital. Rather than 
duplicating capabilities at great cost, efforts should focus on diversification, redundancy, and 
shared technological standards. Emerg ing economies, including Southeast Asia, Latin America, 
and Eastern Europe, can play a role in assembly, testing, and packaging (ATP), enhancing global 
resilience without deepening dependency on any single region. Current projections suggest that 
global semiconductor investments will exceed $2.3 trillion by 2032, with the U.S. sha re of wafer 
fab capacity expected to grow from 10% to 14%. Yet these gains will be meaningful only if they 
are accompanied by parallel advancements in international trust, innovation ecosystems, and labor 
mobility. 
The CHIPS Act, therefore, should be seen not as an endpoint but as a foundational blueprint 
for rebuilding industrial strength within a cooperative, multipolar semiconductor landscape. If the 
United States seeks to lead this sector into the future, it must lead not through isolation or economic 
nationalism, but through openness, shared  innovation, and strategic partnership. By aligning 
capital investment with human capital development and international collaboration, the CHIPS 
Act can evolve from a reactive  industrial measure into a forward -looking instrument of global 
technological resilience. Only by embracing this broader vision can the U.S. semiconductor 
initiative fulfill its promise, not only as a source of national competitiveness, but as a stabilizing 
force in the global economic order.

Policy Responses and National Initiatives 
United States (CHIPS Act) 
• The CHIPS Act (2022) allocates $52 billion to boost domestic semiconductor 
manufacturing. 
• CHIPS for America Fund provides $39 billion in loans and 25% tax credits for 
semiconductor manufacturers. 
• $13 billion is dedicated to research and workforce training. 
• The Chip 4 Alliance (U.S., Japan, Taiwan, South Korea) stabilizes supply chains amid U.S.-
China tensions. 
Taiwan (Advanced Chip Leader) 
• TSMC dominates foundry production, leading in 3 nm and 2 nm technology. 
• $375M annual R&D budget to maintain chip leadership. 
• Tax incentives (25% R&D deduction) and investments in packaging plants. 
Europe (EU Chips Act) 
• $47 billion in public and private investments to double EU’s semiconductor market share 
from 9% to 20% by 2030. 
• Aims to reduce reliance on foreign suppliers and build large -scale chip manufacturing 
capacity. 
China (Semiconductor Self-Sufficiency) 
• Big Fund I ($19.6B) and Big Fund II ($28.9B) drive chip production, design, and 
packaging. 
• Focus on reducing reliance on foreign suppliers, despite sanctions limiting access to 
advanced chipmaking tools. 

• SMIC has developed 7 nm chips using older technology. 
India (Expanding Chip Industry) 
$10 billion initiative for chip design and manufacturing. 
Micron ($825M), Foxconn, and HCLTech investing in chip factories. 
Tower Semiconductor ($8B) and Reliance, Tata Electronics, and Vedanta express interest in 
manufacturing. 
Japan (Economic Security & Chip Production) 
• $7.5 billion investment over five years for semiconductor materials and equipment. 
• Micron ($3.5B) and TSMC ($7B) invest in advanced chip plants. 
• Rapidus (government-backed) is developing 2 nm chips for production by 2025. 
South Korea (K-Chips Act) 
• $229M funds for fabless startups and $1.8B long-term investments. 
• Samsung & ASML invest $230B in new semiconductor plants. 
• Tax incentives (15%-25%) to boost domestic manufacturing. 
Southeast Asia (Growing Semiconductor Hub) 
• Singapore offers tax exemptions and R&D incentives; GlobalFoundries invests $4B in chip 
production. 
• Vietnam receives $2M from the U.S. for workforce training; Samsung, Hana Micron, and 
Amkor investing in semiconductor factories. 
• Malaysia attracts Intel ($5B) and Infineon ($5B) for advanced packaging and silicon 
carbide chip plants. 
• Thailand offers 13-year tax breaks to attract front-end semiconductor companies.
